Zero-Trust Security: A Complete Implementation Guide for Indian Enterprises

15 November 2024 SMTECHHUB Team 12 min read
Introduction

India's digital economy has grown exponentially, but so has its attack surface. With over billions of cyberattacks recorded annually in India alone — a number that has tripled since 2020 — enterprise security teams are stretched thin. The conventional perimeter-based security model, which assumes that everything inside the network is trustworthy, is fundamentally inadequate in a world of cloud workloads, remote workers, and BYOD devices.

Zero-trust architecture is built on the principle of "never trust, always verify." Every user, device, and application must be continuously authenticated and authorised, regardless of location. This isn't a product — it's a strategy.

The Five Pillars of Zero-Trust

1. Identity: Implement strong identity governance using Microsoft Entra ID (formerly Azure AD), Okta, or Ping Identity. Enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for all users. Use Privileged Access Management (PAM) tools like CyberArk to control admin access.

2. Devices: Ensure only compliant, managed devices can access corporate resources. Deploy Mobile Device Management (MDM) solutions like Microsoft Intune or JAMF. Establish device health checks as a condition for access.

3. Network: Replace implicit trust in the internal network with micro-segmentation. Use Zscaler Private Access (ZPA) or Cisco SD-Access to create identity-aware network segments. Implement east-west traffic inspection.

4. Applications: Apply the principle of least-privilege for application access. Use a Software-Defined Perimeter (SDP) to make applications invisible to unauthorised users. Implement Web Application Firewalls (WAF) and API security.

5. Data: Classify data by sensitivity. Apply encryption at rest and in transit. Deploy Data Loss Prevention (DLP) tools to prevent exfiltration.

Implementation Roadmap for Indian Enterprises

Phase 1 (Months 1–3): Identity & Access Management. Begin with enforcing MFA for all users (start with privileged accounts). Implement Single Sign-On (SSO). Conduct an access rights review and clean up stale accounts.

Phase 2 (Months 3–6): Endpoint Security. Deploy EDR on all endpoints. Implement device compliance policies via MDM. Enable conditional access policies tied to device health.

Phase 3 (Months 6–9): Network Segmentation. Map all data flows. Implement micro-segmentation using next-generation firewall policies. Deploy east-west traffic inspection for internal lateral movement detection.

Phase 4 (Months 9–12): Data Protection. Complete data classification exercise. Implement DLP policies across email, endpoints, and cloud. Enable rights management for sensitive documents.

HPE ProLiant vs. Dell PowerEdge: Which Server is Right for Your Data Center?

28 Oct 2024 SMTECHHUB Team
The Server Market in 2024

HPE and Dell collectively account for over 55% of the Indian enterprise server market. Both offer compelling Gen11/Gen16 platforms, but the right choice depends on your workload, management tools, support expectations, and budget. Having deployed hundreds of servers from both vendors, our engineers share an honest comparison.

Performance: Gen11 vs. Gen16

HPE ProLiant Gen11 (released 2023) features Intel Xeon Scalable Gen 4 (Sapphire Rapids) and AMD EPYC Gen 4 options. The DL380 Gen11 remains the best-selling 2U rack server globally. Key differentiator: HPE's Silicon Root of Trust — firmware security baked into silicon.

Dell PowerEdge Gen16 offers similar processor options with Dell's Cyber Resilient Architecture 4.0. The PowerEdge R750 and R650 are strong 2U performers. Dell's OpenManage and iDRAC9 management stack is widely considered more intuitive by many administrators.

VAPT 101: What Every Indian Business Needs to Know

What is VAPT?

VAPT — Vulnerability Assessment and Penetration Testing — is a two-phase security exercise. Phase 1 (VA) uses automated scanning tools to identify known vulnerabilities. Phase 2 (PT) involves ethical hacking to exploit those vulnerabilities and understand the real-world impact.

Types of VAPT

Network VAPT: Tests your network infrastructure — routers, switches, firewalls, servers. Identifies misconfigured services, weak credentials, and unpatched systems.

Web Application VAPT: Tests web applications for OWASP Top 10 vulnerabilities — SQL injection, XSS, CSRF, insecure deserialization, and more.

Mobile App VAPT: Android and iOS application testing for API security, data storage issues, and authentication weaknesses.

Cloud VAPT: Assesses AWS, Azure, or GCP configurations against CIS Benchmarks and cloud security best practices.

VAPT Compliance Requirements in India

RBI mandates annual VAPT for all NBFC and payment system operators. SEBI requires periodic VAPT for market infrastructure institutions. The CERT-In Guidelines 2022 recommend VAPT for critical information infrastructure. ISO 27001 Annex A includes vulnerability management as a mandatory control.

What to Expect in a SMTECHHUB VAPT Report

Our VAPT reports include: Executive Summary (for CXOs), Technical Findings with CVSS scores, Proof-of-Concept evidence screenshots, Risk rating (Critical / High / Medium / Low / Info), and a detailed Remediation Guide with specific fix recommendations.

Wi-Fi 6 vs. Wi-Fi 6E: Which Standard Should Your Enterprise Deploy?

What Changed with Wi-Fi 6E?

Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax) operates on 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands. Wi-Fi 6E extends this to the 6 GHz band — a massive 1,200 MHz of new, uncongested spectrum. In India, TRAI approved 6 GHz spectrum for Wi-Fi 6E in 2023, making it commercially viable for enterprise deployments.

InterferenceModerate (congested 5 GHz)Low (clean 6 GHz)

Our Recommendation

Deploy Wi-Fi 6 now if: You are replacing aging 802.11ac infrastructure, your device fleet is 2–4 years old, and you need cost-effective, high-density wireless coverage.

Deploy Wi-Fi 6E if: You are building a new facility from scratch, your industry requires ultra-low latency (healthcare real-time location, manufacturing OT), or your device fleet is primarily 2024-era capable devices.

The Ultimate Guide to Backup & Disaster Recovery for Indian Businesses

Why Backup Fails When You Need It Most

A large percentage of organizations that experience a major data loss event without a tested DR plan go out of business within 2 years. Yet in our experience, many Indian SMEs have a documented, tested Disaster Recovery Plan. The three most common reasons backup fails: backups were never verified (test restores never run), backup media failed (tape rot, hardware failure), and recovery time was unrealistic (the "backup" was there, but restore took 48+ hours).

The 3-2-1 Backup Rule

Keep 3 copies of data. On 2 different media types (local + cloud, or disk + tape). With 1 copy offsite (cloud or secondary data center). We recommend extending this to 3-2-1-1-0: 3 copies, 2 media, 1 offsite, 1 air-gapped/immutable, 0 errors verified.

RPO and RTO: Know Your Numbers

RPO (Recovery Point Objective): How much data loss is acceptable? "We can afford to lose up to 1 hour of data." Drives your backup frequency. RTO (Recovery Time Objective): How fast must systems be restored? "We must be operational within 4 hours." Drives your infrastructure investment in DR.

Solution Stack We Recommend

Tier 1 (SME): Veeam Backup Essentials + Wasabi S3 cloud storage. Monthly backup testing. RPO: 1 hour, RTO: 4 hours. Cost: ₹8,000–₹15,000/month.

Tier 2 (Mid-Market): Veeam Backup & Replication Enterprise + offsite warm standby server + cloud secondary. RPO: 15 minutes, RTO: 1 hour.

Tier 3 (Enterprise): Veeam + Zerto or Commvault with active-active DC replication. RPO: near-zero, RTO: minutes. Hot/hot DR with automatic failover.
